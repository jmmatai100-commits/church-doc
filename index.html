Foundational Record

What the Book of Mormon is

The Book of Mormon is another testament of Jesus Christ. It is a compilation of records kept by ancient prophets in the Americas from about 600 BC to 400 AD.

It describes Christ visiting the American continent after His Resurrection and teaches that the Bible and Book of Mormon together provide a fuller witness of the Savior.

The book was translated by Joseph Smith by the gift and power of God, and it is meant to accompany the Holy Bible as a testimony of Christ.

Key ideas

  • Ancient prophets kept written records on metal plates.
  • Mormon abridged the record and Moroni finished it.
  • The Book of Mormon supports and clarifies biblical teaching.

Faith & Doctrine

Core LDS teachings explained

Grace and Works

Latter-day Saints believe salvation comes through Christ’s grace, while also honoring the scripture that teaches faith requires action. The Book of Mormon phrase “after all we can do” is understood as meaning “despite what we can do,” not “after earning grace.”

Becoming like God

LDS theology teaches that becoming more like God is a process of growth and maturity, not a competition with God’s supremacy. Scriptures about heirs of God and reigning with Christ support this view.

Temple, family, and sealing

Eternal families and temple ordinances are not meant to replace Christ. They are viewed as part of the fullness of the Gospel — a way of linking generations together in covenant service and worship.

The Godhead

Latter-day Saints teach that the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three distinct beings united in purpose. This is framed as a New Testament view rather than the later Nicene Creed definition of the Trinity.

Evidence & History

Why this matters

Final Thoughts

A message of respect and unity

The document invites readers to approach the LDS Church with humility and curiosity. It emphasizes that people of all faith traditions can work together in the same mission: good vs evil, not denomination vs denomination.

The restored Gospel is presented as a path that brings peace, spiritual growth, and a deeper relationship with Jesus Christ, even if questions remain.
