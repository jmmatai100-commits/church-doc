Very brief intro on what the Book of Mormon even is Since it's clear that most people have only heard of the Book of Mormon and have no clue what it really is or claims to be, I wanted to write this small introduction. Members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS) are nicknamed "Mormons" because of this book. But what even is this book and who is Mormon? The Book of Mormon is a volume of scripture that Latter-day Saints believe is another testament of Jesus Christ. According to the account recorded in the book itself, it is not the writing of one single author but a compilation of records kept by several ancient prophets who lived on the American continent. These prophets recorded their history, teachings, and revelations on metal plates over many centuries from about 600 BC to 400 AD. Within this record is an account of Christ visiting this continent after His Resurrection. As Jesus taught soon before his death in John 10:16, "And other sheep I have, which are not of this fold: them also I must bring, and they shall hear my voice; and there shall be one fold, and one shepherd." Around the fourth century AD, a prophet and military leader named Mormon was entrusted with all of these records. He carefully abridged and organized the writings of earlier prophets into a single record, adding his own commentary and testimony throughout the narrative. Near the end of his life, Mormon passed the plates to his son Moroni. After the destruction of their civilization, Moroni finished the record, wrote the final chapters, and eventually buried the plates in the earth to preserve them for a future generation. As a side note, there is a portion of the Book of Mormon that contains an even older record from a previous civilization who are known as the Jaredites, found in the Book of Ether. This group was believed to have traveled from ancient Mesopotamia to the Americas around the time of the confounding of languages at the Tower of Babel, mentioned in the Bible. According to Latter-day Saint belief, these plates which were compiled by Mormon and buried in the earth by Moroni, were later revealed to Joseph Smith in the early 1800s. Smith translated them into English by the gift and power of God, producing what is now known as the Book of Mormon. This record is meant to go hand in hand with the Holy Bible, as a testimony to the world that Jesus is the Christ and that the fullness of the Gospel has been restored.

Eternal Marriage/Families

The most commonly cited scripture against eternal marriage is Matthew 22:30, where Jesus says, "For in the resurrection they neither marry, nor are given in marriage, but are as the angels of God in heaven." At first glance, this seems to suggest that marriage does not exist in the afterlife. However, context here is very important. Jesus was responding to the Sadducees who did not believe in a resurrection. They thought they had a "gotcha" question for Jesus that proves there can be no resurrection. The Sadducees basically say: "So, a woman gets married and her husband dies without them having any children. Then his brother marries her (as required under Jewish levirate law), but he also dies without any children. And this happens for all seven brothers, and then she dies. So then, who would she be married to in this 'resurrection'?" This is essentially how Jesus responds: "The law already says she's still the wife of the first husband. Do you think people who go to heaven will be jumping from spouse to spouse, following levirate law which only exists in mortality? No. They neither marry or are given in marriage in heaven." Taken in context of what is going on here, this does not disprove the possibility of enduring familial relationships in the eternities. Some church members argue that this question from the sadducees presupposes that Jesus had already taught doctrines pertaining to eternal marriage at some point, which is why they are even mentioning marriage in the resurrection. Galatians Warning

Paul’s warning in Galatians 1:8–9

This is often quoted as if it condemns any future revelation, but the context shows that this is not what Paul was addressing. Paul was writing to the Galatian Christians because certain teachers had come among them claiming that believers must also follow Jewish ceremonial laws, such as circumcision and other requirements of the law of Moses, in order to be saved. His warning about anyone preaching "another Gospel" was directed at those who were changing the Gospel, not at the possibility that God might continue to reveal truth. Great Apostasy

The Great Apostasy/Need for Restoration

Latter-day Saints believe that apostasy occurs when God's priesthood authority and divine guidance are no longer present among His people. Throughout the Bible, there are periods when God's authority and truth were lost because of rebellion, corruption, or the rejection of prophets. After the death of Jesus Christ and His apostles, Latter-day Saints believe that a Great Apostasy took place. During this time, the priesthood authority that Christ had given to His apostles was no longer present on the earth. Many sincere people still desired to follow Jesus Christ and live His teachings, and over time different churches formed as people tried to preserve and interpret the Gospel. However, without the original priesthood authority and with the passing of the apostles, differences in interpretation grew, which is why many Christian denominations today hold differing doctrines about important teachings. Because of this, Latter-day Saints believe that a restoration was necessary. Just as God called prophets in ancient times to restore truth and authority when it had been lost, He once again called a prophet in the latter days. Through Joseph Smith, Latter-day Saints believe the Gospel of Jesus Christ, along with priesthood authority, apostles, prophets, and sacred ordinances, was restored to the earth. In this view, the Restoration was not the creation of a new church, but the reestablishment of the same church Christ originally organized. I went to an old history museum in Provo recently and saw part of the original manuscript that was written out as Joseph Smith spoke the words aloud. We only have about 28% of that original manuscript left. What blew my mind was how it reads like one continuous run-on sentence, with minimal punctuation and no paragraphing. It is exactly what you would expect from a text that was spoken aloud and written down in real time. The scribes were not editing, they were literally writing down the words that were being spoken. All of this happened within a remarkably short window, roughly 60 to 65 working days, moving forward without drafts, outlines, rewrites, or revisions. Once words were spoken, they stayed spoken. The manuscript itself shows no signs of backtracking or restructuring later. Now, think about that book we have today, a 531 page, almost 300,000 word compilation. The Book of Mormon is not a loose collection of ideas or short sermons stitched together. It is a deeply structured work with consistent geography, where directions, distances, and locations that remain coherent across hundreds of pages. It carries long narrative arcs that begin early and resolve much later, with prophecies fulfilled, characters remembered, and consequences carried forward. Doctrine unfolds progressively rather than randomly, building layer upon layer in a way that assumes earlier teachings have not been forgotten. Even more striking, historians and linguists have shown that the major writers in the book speak differently. Nephi does not sound like Alma. Alma does not sound like Mormon. Mormon does not sound like Moroni. Their word choices, rhythms, and emphases are distinct enough that stylometric studies repeatedly identify them as separate voices, showing that they really were different people writing at different times. Then there are the literary features that almost defy explanation in an oral dictation setting. Chiasmus is the most famous; an author will present ideas in one order and then repeat them in reverse order, creating a structure like A, B, C, B, A. This kind of pattern was common in ancient writing because it helped people remember important teachings and made the central idea stand out. The entire chapter of Alma 36 is often pointed to as one of the strongest examples of chiasmus in scripture. The whole chapter forms one large chiasm, where the ideas mirror each other in a structured pattern. But it does not stop there. The text contains extended parallelisms, covenant lawsuit forms, Hebrew style repetition, prophetic judgment language, and carefully framed inclusios that open and close narratives with matching themes and phrases. Some of these structures span dozens of verses or entire chapters. They only become fully visible when you step back and see the whole composition. These are not the kinds of patterns someone casually produces while speaking aloud, without notes, without revision, and without ever stopping to check what was said earlier. Anachronisms

Anachronistic Claims, Archaeology, and Science

Since the moment the Book of Mormon came forth, critics pointed to supposed anachronisms as evidence against its authenticity, arguing that certain claims were thought of as impossible in Joseph Smith's time. History shows that many ideas once thought of as far fetched have become more reasonable and in some cases, astonishing. It is important to understand that faith based texts often describe events that science cannot validate. A great Biblical example of this is Noah's ark and a global flood. Science shows there is no evidence of this happening, but that does not mean it didn't happen. Science works with the evidence it currently has, and its conclusions change as new discoveries are made and this happens all the time. If God truly exists and can act in the world, then it makes sense that some divine events would not fit neatly into present scientific models. With that in mind, it is reasonable to be cautious when calling something in the Book of Mormon an "impossibility." Another example of this is Adam and Eve. Traditional Biblical chronology places them about 4,000 years before Christ, roughly 6,000 years ago. Modern science, using carbon dating, genetics, and archaeology, shows that humans have existed for tens of thousands of years. From a purely scientific standpoint, that creates tension with a literal reading of Genesis. Yet, Christians do not abandon the Bible because of these things. Instead, they allow interpretive space and the understanding that God's ways are not man's ways. Recognizing that ancient texts may not function as modern scientific reports is crucial. The point of this is not to undermine the Bible, but to alleviate the double standard. If believers allow nuance and humility when science challenges Biblical chronology, then supposed anachronisms should not automatically disqualify another ancient religious text such as the Book of Mormon. Science is powerful, but it does not give us a complete picture of the past, nor does it give us a complete picture of how God works. Among the most frequently cited anachronisms in the Book of Mormon are references to animals such as elephants and horses. Critics argue that these animals should not have existed in the Americas during the time periods described, but these conclusions rest on assumptions that are less certain than often presented. Mammoths and mastodons, for example, did inhabit the Americas and belong to the same broader family as elephants, and some evidence suggests they may have overlapped with early human populations longer than once believed. The same idea applies to horses. While mainstream archaeology teaches that horses disappeared from the Americas 10,000 years ago and were later reintroduced by Europeans, that timeline is based on incomplete evidence, and it cannot rule out the possibility of surviving populations or similar species. Ancient records were not written with modern biological classification in mind, and expecting precise scientific language from sacred texts overlooks how people have always named and understood the world around them. It is also reasonable to consider that ancient writers or translators may have used familiar Old World terms to describe New World animals that were similar but not identical. With this being said, here are some supposed Book of Mormon anachronisms, which many still use against the Church today, which have been disproven. Metal plates in a stone box Early critics mocked Joseph Smith's claim of discovering metal plates sealed in a stone box. This sounded like pure fantasy and was a ridiculous thing to say. It had never been heard of. That is until in the mid 1900s, more than a century after Joseph Smith was killed, archaeologists uncovered multiple instances of inscribed metal plates stored in stone containers from the ancient world. Joseph saying that he found inscribed metal plates is one thing, but the detail about them being in a stone box is another. Both of which are now known to be a reality of the ancient world. Lucky guess? Writing on metal in the ancient world (similar to one above) Critics once argued that lengthy sacred records would not have been engraved on metal. Yet discoveries such as ancient silver amulets from Jerusalem containing Biblical text and various bronze and copper inscriptions from antiquity show that metal was indeed used for durable religious writing. This weakens the claim that engraving scripture on metal plates was unheard of or unrealistic in the ancient world. Cement in ancient America The Book of Mormon describes the use of cement in ancient American cities. For years critics dismissed this as an anachronism, insisting that pre Columbian civilizations did not use cement. Later archaeological discoveries in Mesoamerica confirmed that cement like materials were used in construction long before European contact. What had been labeled a clear error turned out to reflect a real ancient technology. Weights and Measures in ancient America The Book of Mormon describes a structured system of weights and measures, most clearly outlined in Book of Mormon, Alma 11:3–19. In this passage, units such as senine, seon, and limnah are used to define the value of gold and silver, which are then tied to everyday goods like grain. This kind of system also appears in other parts of the text where trade, pricing, and exchange are implied, reinforcing the idea that Nephite society operated with an organized economic structure. For many years, critics viewed this as an anachronism, arguing that ancient American civilizations did not have formalized systems of measurement or anything resembling currency. The unfamiliar names of the units and the apparent complexity of the system led some to conclude that it had been invented rather than reflecting a real ancient practice. However, further understanding of ancient economies has shown that many civilizations operated using weight based systems rather than minted coins, where metals were measured and exchanged according to standardized values often tied to goods like grain. Barley in ancient America (connected to point above) For decades, critics confidently claimed that barley did not exist in the Americas before Columbus, yet it is mentioned in the Book of Mormon. This was seen as an anachronism, since "Old World barley" refers to the domesticated species known as Hordeum vulgare, which was widely cultivated in ancient regions like the Near East and Europe and was not believed to be present in the pre-Columbian Americas. However, archaeologists later identified a native form of barley, often called "little barley" (Hordeum pusillum), that was used by indigenous peoples in North America long before European contact. Evidence shows it was cultivated as part of what is sometimes called the Eastern Agricultural Complex, alongside other native crops. This demonstrates that a form of barley did, in fact, exist and was used anciently in the Americas. The earlier categorical claim that barley "never existed" in pre-Columbian America could no longer be sustained. Ancient steel in the Near East/Americas One of the early criticisms of the Book of Mormon centered on the description of the Sword of Laban, which is said to have had a blade "of the most precious steel" in Jerusalem around 600 BC. For many years, critics argued that steel did not exist that early and that such a reference proved the account was historically impossible. Modern archaeological discoveries, however, have shown that forms of hardened iron and early steel were known in parts of the ancient Near East earlier than once assumed. While steel was rare and valuable, its existence in Iron Age Jerusalem is no longer considered implausible. A high quality weapon made of hardened iron or early steel would have fit the social status of someone like Laban. When it comes to metal use in the Americas, the Book of Mormon is more limited than many assume. It mentions gold, silver, copper, brass, iron, and "precious ores," and it records that Nephi made tools and weapons after arriving in the New World. There are also early references to steel among the Jaredites. However, explicit mentions of steel quickly become rare. Later warfare descriptions speak of "swords," cimeters, bows, slings, and shields, but do not consistently specify that the swords were metal. This matters because in ancient Mesoamerica, many tribes used weapons made of wood inset with sharp obsidian blades. The best known example is the macuahuitl, a wooden weapon edged with volcanic glass that was capable of severing limbs. Spanish eyewitnesses called these weapons "swords," and indigenous peoples even referred to Spanish steel blades as "metal macuahuitl," showing that both cultures recognized them as similar categories of weapon. Because the macuahuitl was primarily wood, very few survive archaeologically. It is plausible to say that these were similar to the kind of "swords" that were being used by Book of Mormon civilizations, especially since the mentioning of metals becomes much more rare throughout the record. Complex ancient American civilizations In Joseph Smith's day, many believed Native Americans were incapable of building large scale civilizations. The idea of these great civilizations were thought of as phony. Today, archaeology reveals massive urban centers, sophisticated writing systems, advanced agriculture, and monumental architecture across Mesoamerica where populations are estimated to have been in the millions. This quickly overturned the nineteenth century assumption that such civilizations could not have existed. No Evidence of a massive final Battle We do not actually know with certainty where the Hill Cumorah from the Book of Mormon was located. The hill in upstate New York where Joseph Smith said he found the plates was years later called "Cumorah" by early Church members, but the Book of Mormon itself does not identify that hill as the site of the final Nephite battle. In Mormon 6:6, Mormon says he buried all the records in the hill Cumorah "save it were these few plates which I gave unto my son Moroni," meaning the Book of Mormon plates were not among those buried at the hill cumorah. Because the hill where Josph found the plates was nicknamed cumorah years later, many assume that it's the same cumorah where final battle took place, and argue that there is no evidence of a massive battle happening, but it is not the same hill. Even if we did know where the final battle in the Book of Mormon took place, the absence of bones, weapons, or a clearly marked battlefield does not automatically mean an ancient battle never happened. In many parts of the world, especially in humid or acidic soil conditions, human remains decompose quickly and often leave no trace, even when we know exactly where someone was buried. There are documented battlefields in Europe that are only a few hundred years old, where little to no human remains have been recovered. In tropical regions like parts of Mesoamerica, preservation is even worse. Bodies left exposed, rather than carefully buried, would disappear even faster. Weapons made of wood or other organic materials would also break down over time, leaving little archaeological evidence behind. Archaeology does not preserve most of what once existed; it preserves only a small fraction, usually stone or metal. So the lack of physical remains is not proof that a battle did not occur. It simply reflects the limitations of preservation and recovery in the ancient world. Native American DNA not connected to Near East origin (where Lehi's group came from) It is known that the ancient Book of Mormon civilizations dissolved into what we know as the Native Americans. Many argue that the Book of Mormon is false because Native American DNA has not shown to have Near East origins but traces largely to ancient Asian migrations. It is important to understand that DNA reflects dominant surviving ancestry, not every small group that may have entered a population thousands of years ago. The Book of Mormon does not claim that Lehi's family were the only people in the Americas or the largest group, and if a small Middle Eastern family entered a land already populated by much larger groups, their genetic signature could easily have been absorbed through intermarriage over generations. On top of that, when Europeans arrived in the 1400's and on, disease, warfare, and displacement wiped out up to 90 to 95 percent of Native populations. The created a massive population bottleneck that permanently reshaped the gene pool. When that much of a population disappears, entire genetic lines can vanish with it. Modern DNA studies rely only on surviving markers in present day populations, which means we are working from a dramatically reduced sample of what once existed. For these reasons, DNA is a limited tool for answering ancient historical questions.

Translation

Translation and Modern Language

The Book of Mormon has received heavy criticism for using words that would have not been possible at the time the Book of Mormon was originally written. When ancient records are translated, translators often use translation equivalents, meaning they choose modern words that clearly communicate the original idea to the present audience. This helps explain why the Book of Mormon uses terms like Christ, even though that word comes from Greek and the ancient writers in the Americas would not have spoken Greek. The word Christ simply means "anointed one," the same meaning as the Hebrew word Messiah. When Joseph Smith translated the record into English, he used the word Christ because it is the word modern readers immediately recognize as referring to the Savior. The original writers may have used a completely different word in their language, but if that exact word were left untranslated it would mean nothing to us today. The same principle appears in Bible translation as well, where translators regularly choose modern words that best convey the ancient meaning. In this way, translation is not about preserving every ancient sound, but about communicating the intended meaning clearly to the reader. Many critics point out that the Book of Mormon is written in a style that resembles the King James Bible, using older English phrases like "thee" and "thou," and sounding similar in tone to the New Testament. Joseph Smith was most familiar with the language of the King James Bible, which was the standard scriptural language of his time, so it makes sense that the translation would reflect that style. This idea is actually supported within the Book of Mormon itself, where God declares that He speaks to people "in their own language, unto their own understanding" (2 Nephi 31:3). That principle suggests that divine communication is adapted to the audience receiving it, which would include the language and style most familiar to them.

Priesthood Ban on Black Members

For a period of time in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints had a restriction that prevented men of Black African descent from being ordained to the priesthood and prevented Black members from participating in temple ordinances. I personally do not believe this was inspired by God, nor do many other members of the church. This policy lasted until 1978, when church leaders announced a revelation extending priesthood and temple blessings to all worthy members. It is important to understand that the restriction did not prevent Blacks from joining the Church. Black men and women were baptized, worshipped, served missions in some cases, and lived faithful lives as members even during the years when priesthood ordination and temple participation were restricted. During the first two decades of the Church's existence, a few black men were ordained to the priesthood. One of these men, Elijah Able, also participated in temple ceremonies in Kirtland, Ohio, and was later baptized by proxy for deceased relatives in Nauvoo, Illinois. There is no evidence that any black men were denied the priesthood during Joseph Smith's lifetime. When the policy was first revealed in the 1850s by Brigham Young, he also stated that a future day would come when people of Black African descent would receive the priesthood and temple blessings, showing it was not meant to be permanent. In recent years, the Church has explicitly rejected those earlier racial theories and affirmed that all people are alike unto God. Today, the Church includes a large and expanding Black membership throughout Africa and worldwide. Thousands of congregations across the African continent are led by local members, and many Black Latter-day Saints speak openly about their faith and remaining committed to the Church despite its complicated history. While people may understandably wrestle with the past, many Black members themselves acknowledge the history, choose to stay, and continue to testify of their faith in Jesus Christ and in the restored Gospel.

Multiple Accounts of the First Vision

Joseph Smith taught that when he was about 14 years old, he prayed in a grove of trees near his home in New York because he was confused about religion and wanted to know which church was true. In answer to that prayer, he later testified that God the Father and Jesus Christ appeared to him. Over the course of his life, Joseph shared this experience several times. Because of this, there are multiple accounts of the First Vision, including four written by Joseph himself and several recorded by people who heard him describe it. While the accounts sometimes emphasize different details depending on the audience and the purpose of the telling, they all share the same central message: Joseph prayed sincerely for guidance and received a heavenly answer. Historians, including many who are not members of the Church, generally recognize that variations in the way someone recounts an important personal experience are very normal, especially when those accounts are recorded years apart. When people retell a powerful event from their lives, they often emphasize different details depending on the setting, the audience they are speaking to, or the point they are trying to make. A similar pattern can be seen in the Bible. The conversion of the Apostle Paul on the road to Damascus is recorded three different times in the book of Acts (Acts 9, Acts 22, and Acts 26). Each account describes the same event but includes slightly different details and wording depending on the context in which Paul is telling the story. The same kind of variation appears in the accounts of the Transfiguration of Jesus, which are recorded in Matthew 17, Mark 9, and Luke 9. While each account highlights slightly different elements, they are clearly describing the same event. Differences like these are common in historical records and personal testimonies. Rather than suggesting the stories were invented, small variations often indicate that the accounts were not scripted and were instead shared naturally over time by those who experienced them. Most historians expect some variation when the same event is recorded more than once. What matters most is whether the core story stays consistent, and in the case of the First Vision it clearly does. Across the different accounts, Joseph consistently describes praying for guidance and seeing divine beings who answered his question.

Prophets are imperfect humans

When people study difficult moments in church history, a common question arises: if God truly leads His church, how could imperfect things happen through its leaders? The scriptures show that God has always worked through human beings who were capable of mistakes. Being called as a prophet or apostle does not remove a person's humanity. Throughout the Bible, we see that God accomplishes His work through imperfect individuals. Moses was chosen to lead Israel out of Egypt, yet at one point he disobeyed the Lord by striking the rock when he had been commanded to speak to it (Numbers 20:7–12). Because of this, he was not permitted to enter the promised land. David, one of the greatest kings of Israel, committed serious sins in the incident with Bathsheba. He later repented and continued to be remembered as a man of God. The prophet Jonah initially resisted God's command to preach and attempted to flee before ultimately fulfilling his mission. The New Testament also shows the humanity of Christ's closest followers. The apostle Peter, who would later lead the early church, denied knowing Jesus three times on the night of the Savior's trial. Before Paul's conversion, he was one of the fiercest opponents of the early Christians. He helped persecute followers of Jesus and approved of the death of Stephen. I also think of Peter who began sinking due to lack of faith before the Lord caught him. These examples illustrate a consistent pattern in scripture: God calls real people with weaknesses and imperfections to carry out His purposes. Their humanity does not negate their calling, and their mistakes do not erase the good they accomplish under God's direction. Understanding this pattern can help place difficult moments in religious history into perspective. God works through imperfect people to accomplish His perfect work.
